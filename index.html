Before the library | how a diagnosis is reached

The road to a genetic diagnosis

Reaching a genetic diagnosis can be a single quick test - or a long, iterative search. Knowing the steps helps families ask the right questions and avoid wasted time and money. This is a map, not medical advice; a qualified clinician decides what is actually needed.

Step 1

Clinical clues + family history

A doctor starts with symptoms, examination, and a family tree - especially important where parents are related (consanguinity), which raises the chance of recessive conditions.

Step 2 - the "litmus" tests

Simple, cheap, fast first-line tests

For some conditions one inexpensive test is nearly decisive: a CBC + haemoglobin electrophoresis / HPLC for thalassemia, basic enzyme or metabolite assays for many metabolic disorders, or a karyotype for chromosomal conditions like Down syndrome.

Step 3

Targeted gene or panel testing

If a specific disease is suspected, a single-gene test or a focused NGS panel (a curated group of related genes) is ordered - efficient when the clinical picture points somewhere specific.

Step 4

Chromosomal microarray (CMA)

Detects missing or extra chunks of DNA (deletions / duplications) too small for a karyotype - a common early step for developmental delay or multiple birth differences.

Step 5 - going broad

Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)

When the picture is unclear, multi-system, or doesn't fit one disease, WES reads all ~20,000 protein-coding genes at once. The exome is ~1% of the genome but holds roughly 85% of known disease-causing changes. Bodies such as the ACMG recommend exome / genome as a first- or second-line test for unexplained developmental delay, intellectual disability, or multiple congenital anomalies.

Step 6

Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS)

If WES is negative but a genetic cause is still suspected, WGS reads the entire genome - including non-coding and structural variants the exome can miss.

Step 7

Enzyme & functional assays

To prove a suspected variant truly causes disease, the protein itself is tested - for example measuring enzyme activity (such as low arylsulfatase-A in MLD, or other lysosomal / metabolic enzymes). Low or absent activity confirms the biochemical defect behind the gene change.

Step 8 - novel / unreported variants

Segregation & re-analysis

For a change never reported before, the work turns iterative: segregation testing across affected and unaffected relatives, RNA studies, and re-analysis months later as databases grow. This is how a "variant of uncertain significance" slowly becomes a real answer - and why a novel mutation can take far longer than a textbook one.

Where WES / WGS can be done - by blood sample, abroad or via a national route

Pakistan has limited local exome / genome capacity, so samples are often sent to established international laboratories. Many accept a simple blood sample or a dried-blood-spot card shipped internationally. The cleanest route is through a clinical geneticist (for example via AKU or a PSMG-affiliated clinic), who selects the test, ships the sample, and interprets the report. Examples of reputable international labs:

  • CENTOGENE (Germany) - rare-disease-focused WES / WGS with a worldwide dried-blood-spot service.
  • Blueprint Genetics (Finland, part of Quest) - CAP / ISO-15189 accredited WES, available across many regions including the Middle East and Asia.
  • 3billion (South Korea) - exome sequencing built around rare disease, with free periodic re-analysis.
  • GeneDx (USA) - exome and genome sequencing with one of the largest rare-disease datasets.
  • Baylor Genetics (USA) - long-established clinical exome / genome laboratory accepting international referrals.
  • CeGaT (Germany) - diagnostic exome / genome sequencing that accepts international samples.
  • Fulgent Genetics (USA) - broad exome and panel-testing menu.
Examples for orientation only - not endorsements, and availability, shipping rules and costs change. Patient fees for exome / genome commonly run from a few hundred to several thousand US dollars. Always go through a qualified clinician or geneticist, who can also advise on national or sponsored options and on consent and data handling.

The story behind the name

Aisha's story

Aisha was born on Rare Disease Day in 2019, in Islamabad. In time she was diagnosed with infantile metachromatic leukodystrophy (MLD) - the same rare disease that had already taken her elder sister, Nahl, in 2016.

For Nahl, the diagnosis came too late for treatment to be possible. But because her rare mutation had been identified, doctors were able to recognise Aisha's condition early - early enough for her to travel to Milan and receive trial gene therapy. Today she is walking, and is understood to be the only child from Pakistan to have received this treatment.

Her family now hopes her story can open a door for other Pakistani children who, like Nahl, are too often diagnosed too late.

This is the story that gives our work its name. NAHL carries the name of the sister who was lost; AISHA, our assistant, carries the name of the sister saved by timely knowledge. Between those two names lies our whole purpose - that no family should learn too late what could have been known in time.

HAREEM

HAREEM - Hereditary Awareness for Regenerative and Emerging Engineered Medicine - is the awareness foundation behind ATTYA: a plain-spoken reference on what genetic medicine now makes possible, the toll it takes on families, the economics that exclude the developing world, and why Pakistan must build this capability at home.

THE GENETIC REVOLUTION
Volume I: HAREEM

Hereditary Awareness for Regenerative and Emerging Engineered Medicine

A systemic analysis of global cellular breakthroughs, the multi-dimensional attrition borne by affected families, the granular lifecycle of advanced clinical trials, and the structural economic barriers that necessitate a localized shift toward translational medical sovereignty.

Clinical, financial & molecular rationale | systemic reference framework
Approved Cures: Blood Disorders

First-ever curative platforms in haematology

  • Casgevy (Exagamglogene autotemcel): First-ever FDA/EMA approved CRISPR-Cas9 cellular therapy targeting the BCL11A enhancer. Permanently eliminates painful vaso-occlusive crises in Sickle Cell Disease and eradicates blood transfusion dependence in beta-thalassemia major.
  • Zynteglo (Betibeglogene autotemcel): A lentiviral vector-based ex vivo autologous CD34+ cell therapy that successfully restores functional, continuous beta-globin production in severe thalassemia phenotypes.
Approved Cures: Inherited Blindness

Restoring vision at the genetic source

  • Luxturna (Voretigene neparvovec): An in vivo adeno-associated virus (AAV2) vector therapy delivering functional RPE65 genes directly to subretinal tissue, permanently reversing inherited Leber congenital amaurosis blindness.
Approved Landmarks: Neurodegeneration

Arresting fatal decline of the nervous system

  • Lenmeldy (Atidarsagene autotemcel): Landmark 2024 approved ex vivo autologous hematopoietic stem cell gene therapy. Delivers functional ARSA genes to arrest the rapid, fatal neurodegeneration of infantile or juvenile Metachromatic Leukodystrophy (MLD).
  • Kebilidi (Eladocagene exuparvovec): An in vivo AAV2 vector carrying a human dopa decarboxylase gene, infused neurosurgically directly into the brain structure. First gene therapy approved for AADC deficiency.
Approved Landmarks: Sickling & Bleeding

Next-generation vectors in blood disease

  • Lyfgenia (Lovotibeglogene autotemcel) & Beqvez (Fidanacogene elaparvovec): Advanced lentiviral and next-gen AAV vectors. Lyfgenia prevents cellular sickling via a modified gene, while Beqvez provides multi-year Factor IX expression to halt Hemophilia B bleeds.
Active Trials: Neurodegenerative & Systemic

Silencing toxic proteins, delivering by breath

  • Huntington's Disease (Targeted Gene Silencing): Phase I/II clinical trials utilize antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) and AAV vectors carrying microRNA (miRNA) to directly degrade the toxic, mutant Huntingtin (mHTT) protein before it destroys cortical and striatal neurons.
  • Cystic Fibrosis (Inhalation Gene Delivery): Phase II evaluations testing non-viral lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) and engineered lentiviral vectors delivered via nebulizers to transport functional CFTR genes directly to respiratory epithelial cells.
Active Trials: Muscle Integrity

Sustaining cardiac and skeletal structure

  • Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (Next-Gen Mini-Dystrophin): Expanding Phase III trials evaluating optimized mini-dystrophin vector designs across broader age brackets to sustain long-term cardiac and skeletal muscle structural integrity.
Active Trials: Metabolic Errors

Restoring missing enzymes and pathways

  • Fabry and Gaucher Disease (Ex Vivo & In Vivo Regimes): Active Phase I/II global clinical trials utilizing liver-targeted AAV vectors or gene-edited bone marrow stem cells to induce permanent high-level expression of missing alpha-galactosidase A or glucocerebrosidase enzymes.
  • Phenylketonuria (PKU): Phase I/II trials targeting adult profiles using liver-directed AAV vectors to reintroduce the missing PAH gene, permanently lifting extreme, lifelong dietary restrictions.
Active Trials: Developmental Targets

Correcting early synaptic arrest

  • Rett Syndrome & Fragile X: Early-stage Phase I trials carefully escalating micro-dosing schemas to introduce tightly regulated MECP2 gene constructs, aiming to correct severe early-childhood developmental and synaptic arrest.
The Triad of Attrition (1 of 2)

The multi-dimensional toll on families

  • Physical burden. Perpetual operational routines. Families manage bi-weekly painful hospital infusions, rigorous iron-chelation schedules, or watch irreversible muscle wastage and neurological decline advance daily.
  • Emotional burden. The psychological toll of chronic fatal diagnoses. Parents face constant survival anxiety, sibling care neglect, profound guilt over hereditary genetic transmission, and raw trauma.
The Triad of Attrition (2 of 2)

The multi-dimensional toll on families

  • Financial burden. Total economic exhaustion. Lifelong palliative requirements consume daily disposable income, deplete generational assets, restrict career advancement, and trigger family bankruptcies.
The Capital Engine (1 of 2)

Why a cure costs billions

Discovery & Basic R&D (~$1.5 Billion) -> GMP Scale-up & Validation (~$1.5 Billion) -> Scarce Patient Cohort Trials (~$2.0 Billion)

  • The 15-Year Timeline: Sourcing optimized vectors, configuring precision payloads, and executing sequential human clinical trials demands an absolute minimum of 10 to 15 years.

The Chronic-Delivery Exception

When "one-time" becomes a lifetime

The chronic delivery exception (Vyjuvek): while topical applications for Epidermolysis Bullosa cost roughly $25,000 per dose, the required weekly lifetime regimen pushes projected long-term patient exposure costs to $15-$22 million.
  • A "cheaper" per-dose price can still become one of the most expensive treatments of all - because, unlike a single curative infusion, it must be re-applied for life.
Pricing Strategy: Rationale

The Western "value-based" pricing model

  • The Corporate Justification: Western pharmaceuticals calculate multi-million dollar tags using a "value-based pricing" model. They argue a single $3M injection is cost-effective because it cancels out $4M to $6M in lifetime chronic transfusions and hospital care.
  • Amortizing Small Markets: Because rare diseases affect relatively small paper footprints, companies hyper-inflate the per-dose costs to recoup their upfront $5 billion investments within a narrow patent window.
Pricing Strategy: The Flaw

Where the model breaks down

  • The Capitalist Blind Spot: This approach assumes the existence of high-income, heavily subsidized Western insurance frameworks. It completely breaks down in developing economies where healthcare is paid entirely out-of-pocket.
The Pakistan Paradox (1 of 2)

Why Western commercial models exclude us

  • The Genetic Density Crisis: Due to deeply entrenched systemic and cultural consanguineous marriage patterns across Pakistan, the prevalence of inherited monogenic disorders - such as Beta-Thalassemia Major and severe neurodevelopmental deficiencies - is among the highest in the world.
  • The Income Gap Absurdity: The average monthly household income across Pakistan is roughly PKR 45,000 to 75,000. The cost of a single dose of Zynteglo is $2.8 Million USD (approx. 800 Million PKR).
The Pakistan Paradox (2 of 2)

Why Western commercial models exclude us

  • The Reality of Exclusion: A multi-million-dollar price tag is functionally infinite for a local citizen. Relying on imported commercial pharmacology means completely abandoning millions of children to early mortality.
The Rationale for ATTYA Pakistan (1 of 2)

Healthcare through academic sovereignty

  • De-Commercializing the Molecule: By shifting advanced genetic medicine from a venture-capital, for-profit commercial model to a state-backed academic consortium, we completely eliminate the massive corporate profit margins driving Western prices.
  • Repurposing Existing Assets: Establishing localized, domestic viral vector synthesis, cell-processing cleanrooms, and clinical trials within Pakistan's public universities and state medical institutions cuts development infrastructure costs by over 85%.
The Rationale for ATTYA Pakistan (2 of 2)

Healthcare through academic sovereignty

  • The Ultimate Cost Reductions: While imported Western treatments cost $2,800,000 USD per patient, the ATTYA Indigenous Model projects a cost of <$35,000 USD focused strictly on raw materials, local cleanroom operations, and baseline support.
Clinical Gateways: Screening (1 of 2)

Mitigating trial risk before enrolment

  • Inclusion Criteria Foundations: Requires absolute, definitive molecular and next-generation sequencing confirmation of the targeted monogenic mutation. Enrolment must happen before irreversible end-organ or systemic tissue fibrosis sets in.
  • The Exclusion Barriers: Pre-existing neutralizing antibody titers (e.g., anti-AAV capsids) which instantly clear the viral vectors before they target host tissue. Advanced systemic hepatic or renal failure also bars candidates due to baseline conditioning toxicity risks.
Clinical Gateways: Screening (2 of 2)

Mitigating trial risk before enrolment

  • The Registry Framework: Establishing centralized, standardized national registries to aggregate and identify clear eligible cohorts across Pakistan's diverse medical network.
The Trial Lifecycle (1 of 2)

De-mystifying the operational protocol

Genomic Screening & Consent -> Conditioning / Bone Marrow Ablation -> Precision Vector Infusion Window -> 15-Year Longitudinal Follow-up
  • Informed Consent & Genotyping: In-depth counseling explaining the permanent, life-altering nature of genomic alteration to families.
  • Conditioning & Harvesting: Preparing the cellular macro-environment (e.g., bone marrow clearance via targeted chemotherapy for blood disorders) or administering transient immunosuppression.
The Trial Lifecycle (2 of 2)

De-mystifying the operational protocol

  • Precision Infusion Window: Delivering the payload within ultra-sterile, site-specific environments (such as dedicated bone marrow transplant units or highly specialized operating rooms).
  • Long-Term Surveillance: Transitioning from acute post-infusion toxicity monitoring to a mandatory 15-year long-term follow-up loop to verify expression durability and check for delayed genomic toxicities.
Delivery Frameworks: In Vivo

Direct systemic delivery

  • In Vivo Model (Direct Systemic Delivery):
    The Process: The therapeutic gene payload is packaged directly into a viral vector and injected straight into the patient's body (intravenously, intrathecally, or subretinally).
    Primary Vector: Adeno-Associated Virus (AAV).
    Targets: Non-dividing, long-lived tissues such as the liver, central nervous system, and retina.
Delivery Frameworks: Ex Vivo

Cellular engineering outside the body

  • Ex Vivo Model (Cellular Engineering Outside the Body):
    The Process: Patient stem cells are harvested, engineered inside a sterile, localized GMP cleanroom laboratory, validated, and then re-infused into the patient.
    Primary Vector: Lentivirus or Retrovirus.
    Targets: Rapidly dividing systems, primarily hematopoietic stem cells (bone marrow) and T-cells.
Molecular Engines: Editing (1 of 2)

From gene replacement to precision edits

  • CRISPR-Cas9 (The Molecular Scissors): Uses a guide RNA sequence to make targeted double-stranded breaks in the DNA. This approach can either completely "knock out" a faulty gene or allow template-directed repairs.
  • Base Editing (The Molecular Pencil): Chemically modifies a single nucleotide base pair (e.g., converting a C to a T) without breaking the DNA backbone. This dramatically lowers the risk of random insertion or deletion errors.
Molecular Engines: Editing (2 of 2)

From gene replacement to precision edits

  • Prime Editing (The Search-and-Replace Word Processor): An advanced technology capable of writing entirely new genetic sequences directly into specified genomic sites without requiring double-stranded DNA breaks or donor DNA templates.
The Biological Ledger: Pros

The curative upside

  • The Crucial Pros (The Upside):
    The Curative Paradigm: Corrects the root molecular cause of a disease, replacing decades of painful, lifestyle-limiting palliative management with a single definitive intervention.
    Target Precision: Patches or modifies specific corrupted gene segments while leaving healthy surrounding DNA intact.
The Biological Ledger: Cons

The biological risks

  • The Crucial Cons (The Biological Risks):
    Immunogenicity: Intense immune reactions where the host body flags viral vectors or bacterial proteins as hostile, triggering severe systemic inflammation.
    Off-Target Mutations & Insertional Mutagenesis: The risk of gene editing tools interacting with unintended genomic areas, potentially disrupting vital genes or activating oncogenes.

Gene therapy, in plain terms

The idea behind a one-time cure

  • Treat the cause, not the symptom. Most inherited diseases come from a single faulty instruction in a personâ€™s DNA. Conventional medicine manages the downstream damage for life; gene therapy aims to correct or replace that faulty instruction itself - ideally just once.
  • Two ways to deliver it. Either a corrected gene is carried into the body by a harmless, engineered virus (in vivo), or a patientâ€™s own cells are collected, fixed in a lab, and returned (ex vivo).
How the field grew up

From risky experiment to approved medicine

  • From setbacks to science. The first gene-therapy trials in the 1990s met serious safety setbacks. The field then spent two decades making its delivery viruses far safer, more targeted and more predictable.
  • From experimental to approved. The late 2010s brought the first regulator-approved gene therapies - for inherited blindness and spinal muscular atrophy - and by the early 2020s, CRISPR-based cures for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia had reached patients.
Why Pakistan is fertile ground

The opportunity, stated plainly

  • The need is already here. A high burden of inherited disease and a large, young patient population place the demand for curative genetic medicine among the highest anywhere in the world.
  • The building blocks exist. Transplant units, university genomics labs and oncology cell-biology facilities already perform much of the underlying work. What is missing is coordination and intent - not raw capability.
The demographic imperative (1 of 2)
  • Consanguinity. An estimated 60-70% of marriages in Pakistan are consanguineous, driving a high prevalence of autosomal-recessive conditions.
  • Thalassemia burden. Roughly 5,000-9,000 children are born with beta-thalassemia major each year, with a standing patient base above 100,000 dependent on lifelong transfusions.
The demographic imperative (2 of 2)
  • Neuromuscular reality. Thousands of children face degenerative conditions such as SMA - largely under-reported, and without advanced local treatment.
  • The cost of waiting. Every year without local capability means another cohort of children managed palliatively - while curative science already exists elsewhere.

Caring for the carer

A caregiver's rights & responsibilities

Walking with a child through a rare disease asks everything of a family. You carry the load best when you know what you can expect from others - and what helps your child most. This is general, non-medical guidance, offered with care.

Your rights
  • To clear, honest information in a language you understand, and the time to absorb it.
  • To ask questions, seek a second opinion, and be a partner in every decision about your child.
  • To access your child's records, reports and test results.
  • To be treated with respect and dignity - never judged for what you do not yet know.
  • To your own health and rest; caring for yourself is part of caring for them.
  • To emotional support and the company of families who understand.
  • To say "I need help" without shame, and to lean on family, community and faith.
Your responsibilities
  • To share an accurate history and your honest observations with the care team.
  • To follow agreed care plans as best you can, and to speak up when something isn't working.
  • To keep records organised - reports, medicines, appointments and questions.
  • To learn enough to partner with clinicians, without trying to replace them.
  • To protect your child's dignity, privacy and comfort at every step.
  • To watch for your own burnout and seek respite before you are empty.
  • To hold on to hope and patience - for your child, and for yourself.
You cannot pour from an empty cup. Rest, ask for help, and accept it - a steadier carer is the greatest gift to a child who depends on you.

About us

A letter from our founder

I did not choose this work; it chose me the day a doctor spoke a word I could not spell, and my world changed forever.

My daughter, Nahl, was radiant with laughter until a rare inherited illness slowly took her voice, her steps, her sight — and finally, her breath. We searched every page, knocked on every door, but by the time we understood the illness, it had already claimed her. She left too soon, leaving a silence that will never fully lift.

Yet she also left us a gift. Because of what we learned too late for her, we tested in time for her sister. Aisha is alive today — growing, arguing, gloriously herself — because knowledge arrived early enough to matter. That difference, between too late and in time, is why this organization exists.

NAHL carries the name of the daughter we lost. AISHA, our companion tool, carries the name of the daughter we saved. Between those names lives our purpose: that no parent should learn at a graveside what a simple test could have told them at a cradle.

We are not a hospital, and we hold no cure. What we offer is what I once begged for and could not find: clear answers, a map through the fear, the names of those who can truly help, and the steady hand of someone who has walked this road and is still standing.

If you have just been handed news too heavy to carry, hear me gently: you are not alone, you did nothing to cause this, and for the children still with you, it is not too late to act.

For Nahl, whom we could not save. For Aisha — and for your child — whom we still can.
